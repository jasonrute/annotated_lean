  9  /--
 10  Reducibility hints are used in the convertibility checker.
 11  When trying to solve a constraint such a
 12  
 13             (f ...) =?= (g ...)
 14  
 15  where f and g are definitions, the checker has to decide which one will be unfolded.
 16    If      f (g) is opaque,     then g (f) is unfolded if it is also not marked as opaque,
 17    Else if f (g) is abbrev,     then f (g) is unfolded if g (f) is also not marked as abbrev,
 18    Else if f and g are regular, then we unfold the one with the biggest definitional height.
 19    Otherwise both are unfolded.
 20  
 21  The arguments of the `regular` constructor are: the definitional height and the flag `self_opt`.
 22  
 23  The definitional height is by default computed by the kernel. It only takes into account
 24  other regular definitions used in a definition. When creating declarations using meta-programming,
 25  we can specify the definitional depth manually.
 26  
 27  For definitions marked as regular, we also have a hint for constraints such as
 28  
 29            (f a) =?= (f b)
 30  
 31  if self_opt == true, then checker will first try to solve (a =?= b), only if it fails,
 32  it unfolds f.
 33  
 34  Remark: the hint only affects performance. None of the hints prevent the kernel from unfolding a
 35  declaration during type checking.
 36  
 37  Remark: the reducibility_hints are not related to the attributes: reducible/irrelevance/semireducible.
 38  These attributes are used by the elaborator. The reducibility_hints are used by the kernel (and elaborator).
 39  Moreover, the reducibility_hints cannot be changed after a declaration is added to the kernel.
 40  -/
 41  inductive reducibility_hints
 42  | opaque  : reducibility_hints
 43  | abbrev  : reducibility_hints
 44  | regular : nat → bool → reducibility_hints
